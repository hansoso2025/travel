Saturday 4 July — land, eat, wander, San Lorenzo

The day in one paragraph

You land at 10:00 and reach the hostel around 11:30. Drop the bag (ask for the lower bunk) and stay out, you do not need to go back and rest, and you check in whenever you drift past. Start with a proper local lunch at Trattoria Monti, look into Santa Maria Maggiore next door (free), then wander the lanes of Monti. Since free revisits are fair game, keep going on foot down Via dei Fori Imperiali and see the Fori and the Colosseum from the outside (no ticket), and drift into the Old Town if you feel like it. In the evening, San Lorenzo, the student quarter you did not see in 2024, for the food.

Sunday 5 July — centre, Trastevere, market, Testaccio

The day in one paragraph

A flat, food-first day as one straight north-to-south sweep, always heading south with no doubling back. Start in the centre while it is quiet: the Jewish Ghetto, then Tiber Island and across to Trastevere's quiet western lanes (a small Otaleg gelato on the way). Walk south to the Porta Portese Sunday flea market, then a proper Roman lunch at La Tavernaccia da Bruno right beside it. Cross Ponte Sublicio into Testaccio, Rome's real food quarter, for the afternoon: the streets, the Pyramid of Cestius, a coffee or granita, and the food-finale dinner at Flavio al Velavevodetto. Two proper meals in two different areas, snacks in between, and the day ends where you finish. (Garbatella was dropped, a southern metro spur that did not earn the detour.)

Tickets, transport, practicalities

  • No paid entries, by design. Everything here is free: the basilica, the markets, the neighbourhoods, the Colosseum and Pyramid from outside, Tiber Island. No monument tickets, no museums, nothing you saw in 2024 or that the offsite will cover.
  • Transport. Airport: Leonardo Express (FCO↔Termini, ~32 min, ~€14). In town: contactless tap (~€1.50 / 100 min). Sat: walking plus a tram/metro hop to San Lorenzo. Sun: mostly walking (centre to Trastevere to the market to Testaccio), Tram 3/8 links Testaccio back to the centre; both river crossings are on foot (over Tiber Island to Trastevere, then back at Ponte Sublicio into Testaccio).
  • Booking. Reserve Trattoria Monti (Sat lunch), Pommidoro (Sat dinner), La Tavernaccia da Bruno (Sun lunch) and Flavio al Velavevodetto (Sun dinner). Pizzeria Formula 1 is walk-in; Perilli is the Sunday-dinner fallback.
  • Heat. ~31-33°C. Your umbrella covers the sun; keep water topped up at the nasoni (street fountains). Churches want shoulders and knees covered.

Background: what to eat, and what these dishes actually are

Reference, not a schedule. Roman food is cucina povera, poor-kitchen cooking that turns cheap cuts and pantry staples into the city's signature plates. The offsite gives you a pizzeria (Mon) and a pasta-and-tiramisu class (Tue), so this weekend leans into street food, offal and sweets.

Savoury

  • Supplì — a fried rice ball, risotto rice bound with tomato ragù around a molten mozzarella centre. Eaten by hand.
  • Pizza al taglio / pizza romana — "by the cut" from a tray, sold by weight, or the thin crisp round Roman pizza.
  • Filetti di baccalà — salt cod fillets in batter, deep-fried. A classic fritto.
  • The four Roman pastas — carbonara (egg, guanciale, pecorino, pepper, no cream), cacio e pepe (pecorino and pepper), amatriciana (tomato, guanciale, pecorino), gricia (the white, tomato-less one). Guanciale is cured pork cheek.
  • Coda alla vaccinara — oxtail slow-braised with celery and tomato, the definitive Testaccio dish from your 2024 list.
  • Rigatoni con la pajata / coratella — pasta with a milk-fed calf's intestines (pajata); coratella is heart, lung and offal with artichokes. Traditional, not for the squeamish.
  • Carciofi alla romana / giudia — artichokes braised with mint (romana) or flattened and deep-fried whole, Jewish-Roman style (giudia).
  • Trapizzino — a pocket of pizza bread stuffed with a spoonful of a classic stew. A modern Roman street-food invention, born in Testaccio.

Sweet

  • Maritozzo con panna — a soft sweet bun split and packed with whipped cream. The Roman breakfast treat; Regoli is the benchmark.
  • Crostata alle visciole — a lattice tart of sour wild cherries, a Jewish-Ghetto speciality (you had this in 2024).
  • Gelato — look for natural colours and seasonal fruit, the sign of in-house production. Otaleg qualifies.

Non-alcoholic drinks to try

  • Caffè freddo — pre-sweetened cold espresso, everywhere in summer. Granita di caffè con panna is shaved coffee ice with cream.
  • Spremuta — fresh-squeezed juice. Chinotto is a dark, bitter-citrus soda.
  • Cappuccino is a morning drink by local custom; after lunch, an espresso. Tap and nasone water is safe and cold.

Notes and honest caveats

  • Each day runs one direction. Saturday flows out from the hostel through Monti and the Old Town to San Lorenzo. Sunday is a straight north-to-south sweep (centre, Trastevere, market, Testaccio), crossing to the west bank at the island and back at Ponte Sublicio, always heading south, dinner where you end. No doubling back.
  • All food picks vetted to your standard, and their weekend hours confirmed. Popular but the substance holds: Trattoria Monti (Sat lunch), Pommidoro (Sat dinner), Otaleg (gelato), Flavio al Velavevodetto (Sun dinner in Testaccio; like Perilli, a known name with a tourist mix but the food holds).
