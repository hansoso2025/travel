Short answer: yes, by about ¥7,700 plus free seat reservations, if you activate it on the right day. Here is the audit.

  • Why Day 5 to 9. That window straddles the four priciest legs, above all the ¥15,500 Kagoshima-to-Beppu run. No other 5 consecutive days catch as much.
  • What stays separate (it falls outside the window, on Days 3, 10, 11): leg 2 Fukuoka→Nagasaki (~¥6,000), leg 7 Beppu→Yufuin (~¥1,100), leg 8 Yufuin no Mori (~¥5,000). Total ~¥12,100. No single 5-day window can hold these and the big legs at once, so paying them point-to-point is correct.
  • Total rail spend: ¥24,000 pass + ~¥12,100 separate = ~¥36,100, versus ~¥43,800 if every leg were bought point-to-point. Two passes would cost ¥48,000, more than buying everything separately, so one well-timed pass is the play.
  • Activation: you choose the start date; it runs 5 consecutive calendar days; first use is the Day 5 morning train out of Nagasaki.
  • Reservations: with the pass you can just board the non-reserved car of any regular limited express or bullet train, no booking needed. Reserved seats are free and unlimited, bookable online in advance (JR Kyushu pass booking site, open 05:30-23:00 Japan time) or at any station, not in person only. The two sightseeing trains (Tamatebako, Yufuin no Mori) are 100% reserved with no non-reserved car, so you must hold a reservation to board, pass or not; book those about a month ahead.

FUKUOKA · Days 1-2 · arrival hub
Theme

Kyushu's biggest, most easygoing city. A food-and-neighbourhood place, light on big monuments, so it works as a gentle reset after the flight.

How the days split

Day 1 (Sun) arrives in the morning and uses the afternoon for Dazaifu plus the Kyushu National Museum, which is open on Sundays. Day 2 (Mon) is central Fukuoka, since the museum closes Mondays. You get the city again at the relaxed end, Days 11-12.

Route

Local food primer - Fukuoka (what is famous here)

Hakata ramen (tonkotsu): thin, straight noodles in a rich, milky broth made from boiled pork bones; the city's signature bowl. Motsunabe: a hotpot of beef or pork offal (intestines) simmered with cabbage and garlic chives. Mizutaki: a gentle, clear chicken hotpot. Mentaiko: spicy-marinated pollock fish roe (the eggs), eaten with rice or in pasta. Yatai: the open-air street-food stalls at Nakasu, serving ramen, grilled skewers, and oden (bits simmered in broth). Umegae mochi: a grilled rice cake, crisp outside, with sweet red-bean paste inside (a Dazaifu snack). Sweet: Hakata Torimon, a soft cake with a milky white-bean filling; and amaou, a prized local strawberry. Non-alcoholic drink: Yame gyokuro, a top shaded green tea from Fukuoka's Yame area, mellow and a little sweet.

Lucky timing: the Nagasaki Kunchi festival (7-9 Oct), and Day 4 is its opening day

"Kunchi" (くんち) is Nagasaki's big autumn festival, held every year on 7, 8 and 9 October at Suwa Shrine and a few other venues around town. It is famous for its blend of Japanese, Chinese and Dutch influences, a legacy of Nagasaki's trading-port past. Each year a handful of neighbourhoods (the odori-cho, "dance towns") take turns performing in a rotation, so each one comes round only once every seven years. The signature acts are the ja-odori (a long dragon, carried and spun by a team) and elaborate wheeled floats (kasaboko and decorated "ships"). 2026 features six dance towns, including a dragon dance and the famous "Kokko-desho" float.

  • Free to enjoy. The paid grandstand seats (sajiki) inside Suwa Shrine are limited and tend to sell out months ahead, so don't count on them. You don't need them: during all three days the troupes do niwasaki-mawari, roving performances through the city centre (the Hamamachi arcade, around Suwa Shrine, shopfronts and squares), free to watch on the street.
  • How to use it. Treat the Day 4 afternoon and evening as festival time. Check the day's niwasaki-mawari route locally (the tourist office and festival site publish a timetable) and position yourself where the dragon and floats pass. The mood runs late, which suits your late-dinner habit.
  • Knock-on for booking. Kunchi packs the city, so Nagasaki lodging for 6-8 Oct will fill and rise; book it early (flagged in the checklist).
Note: per your steer the wartime sites (Peace Park, Atomic Bomb Museum) are left out. If you ever want them back, they would slot into the Day 4 late morning, but the day is now full with Gunkanjima and the festival.
Tickets / passes: Glover ¥620, Dejima ~¥520, Mt Inasa ropeway ~¥1,250, Gunkanjima cruise ~¥3,600 to 4,500. A 1-day tram pass (¥600) covers the in-city hops. Fukuoka-to-Nagasaki is point-to-point (before the pass window).
Local food primer - Nagasaki (what is famous here)

Champon: thick noodles in a milky pork-and-seafood broth, piled with pork, squid, and cabbage; invented here. Sara udon: the same toppings as a gravy, poured over crispy fried noodles. Toruko (Turkish) rice: one plate holding pilaf rice, a breaded pork cutlet, and spaghetti side by side; a local oddity with no real link to Turkey. Castella (kasutera): a moist honey sponge cake the Portuguese brought over 400 years ago, sold in boxes; the city's iconic sweet. Shippoku: a shared banquet that mixes Japanese, Chinese, and European dishes on one table. Non-alcoholic drink: the Nagasaki "milk shake," a thick, frozen, almost soft-serve drink you eat with a spoon.

Local food primer - Kumamoto (what is famous here)

Basashi: raw horse meat, sliced thin like sashimi, eaten with soy sauce, ginger and garlic; the local delicacy (yes, horse). Background, in plain words

Reference only, parked at the end so it stays out of the way of the planning content. Quick context for the terms and places above.

Sports Day (Mon 12 Oct 2026)

A Japanese national holiday on the second Monday of October (it began to mark the 1964 Tokyo Olympics). Falling on a Monday, it makes a 3-day weekend, one of autumn's busiest domestic travel windows. Effect on us: bullet trains, the reserved sightseeing trains, and popular onsen towns and hotels all fill up. That is why the plan keeps you in one Kagoshima hotel across that weekend and books a few seats and that hotel early.

Gunkanjima "landing"

Gunkanjima ("Battleship Island," officially Hashima) is an abandoned coal-mining island about 18 km off Nagasaki; its concrete ruins and silhouette look like a warship, and it is now a UNESCO site. Tour boats go out; "landing" means the boat actually docks so you walk on a fixed observation deck, versus only circling the island. The dock faces open sea, so a safe landing is only possible on calm days (around 100 a year). You book the cruise; the landing is the bonus if the water is calm.

Onsen vs the "hells"

An onsen is a natural hot-spring bath you soak in, usually at a ryokan (a traditional inn) that also serves a multi-course kaiseki dinner. Beppu's "hells" (jigoku) are the opposite: geothermal ponds near 100°C, in vivid colours (cobalt blue, blood red), far too hot to enter. They are a viewing circuit. So in Beppu and Yufuin you both soak (onsen) and sightsee (the hells).

Sakurajima and the ferry

Sakurajima is one of Japan's most active volcanoes, sitting in the bay directly across from Kagoshima city, and it regularly dusts the city with ash. You reach it by a short public ferry (15 min, every 15 to 20 min, around the clock) because it is across the water. You cannot climb it (a safety zone bans entry within 2 km of the crater), so you take it in from observatories like Yunohira, linked by the Island View loop bus.

Sightseeing trains (and why they must be reserved)

Beyond ordinary trains, JR Kyushu runs themed "sightseeing trains" with designed interiors and big panoramic windows; the ride itself is the attraction. Two are on this trip: the forest-green Yufuin no Mori (Yufuin to Fukuoka, Day 11) and the black-and-white Ibusuki no Tamatebako (to the sand baths, Day 8). Important: these are fully reserved, with no non-reserved car, so you must hold a seat reservation to board at all, even with the rail pass, and they sell out. Book about a month ahead.

Bullet train, limited express, and the "relay"

The Shinkansen is the high-speed bullet train. A "limited express" (like the Sonic to Beppu, or the Kirishima) is a fast ordinary train, a notch slower but still quick. One quirk: the Nagasaki bullet line is not yet joined to the main network, so Fukuoka to Nagasaki means a normal express to Takeo-Onsen, then a change (the "relay") onto the bullet train for the last stretch.

Rail pass and reserved seats, in one line

A rail pass is prepaid unlimited travel on JR trains for a set number of consecutive days. A "reserved seat" is a guaranteed assigned seat; "non-reserved" is first-come in open cars. With the All-Kyushu pass you board non-reserved cars freely, and reserved seats are free and unlimited (book online or at a station). The full cost breakdown is in the "Getting around" section near the top.

Nishitetsu (the Dazaifu line)

Fukuoka has a private railway, Nishitetsu, separate from JR. Dazaifu sits on it, so the JR rail pass does not cover that trip; it is a small separate fare. Noted so the pass math stays clear.

Why Nagasaki has Dutch and church history

For about 200 years Japan was largely closed to the world. Nagasaki was the one exception: Dejima, a small fan-shaped artificial island, was the only place Dutch traders could live, with a Chinese quarter nearby. After Japan reopened in the 1850s, Western merchants built hillside mansions (now Glover Garden) and churches (Oura, Japan's oldest). That history is why Nagasaki feels different, and why its food (champon, castella) does too.

Daimyo gardens, and what Suizenji and Sengan-en actually are

Until 1868 Japan was "feudal": the country was split into territories, each run by a powerful regional lord (a daimyo) who controlled the land and the samurai under him, all beneath a top military ruler (the shogun). A "daimyo garden" is a large landscape garden a lord built beside his residence, made for strolling. Suizenji (full name Suizenji Jojuen) is the name of one such garden in Kumamoto, not a person. Its quirk: it shrinks scenery from the old Tokaido highway (the historic road linking Edo, today's Tokyo, with Kyoto, lined with 53 post towns) into miniature inside the garden, including a small grassy mound shaped like Mt Fuji. So it is a garden of miniature landscapes, not a model town and not a person. Sengan-en is the name of the Shimazu family's villa-and-garden estate in Kagoshima. It uses "borrowed scenery" (shakkei): instead of walling itself off, it frames the real Sakurajima volcano across the bay so the volcano reads as part of the garden.

Why the Meiji Restoration keeps coming up in Kagoshima

This is here only because three of your Kagoshima stops make more sense with one fact. Until 1868 Japan was run by a military government (the shogun), with the emperor a figurehead. Kagoshima, then the Satsuma domain ruled by the Shimazu lord family, led the rebellion that toppled the shogun and handed power back to the emperor, the upheaval that launched Japan's rapid modernisation, known as the "Meiji Restoration." That one thread links your stops: Sengan-en is the Shimazu family's estate, and Shiroyama hill is where the last samurai revolt made its final stand a few years later. (The Reimeikan history museum tells the fuller story, but you've dropped it from the plan, since Sengan-en and Shiroyama already carry it; keep it only as a wet-weather fallback.) If that history does not grab you, Shiroyama's view alone still earns the stop.

Tax-free and Visit Japan Web

Foreign tourists can buy goods (like the Uniqlo haul) without Japan's consumption tax above a small threshold by showing a passport. "Visit Japan Web" is the government site you pre-register on for smooth immigration, customs, and tax-free processing.
